01

Introduction

Virtual Reality (VR) enables immersive, interactive manufacturing environments for training and system monitoring, and Digital Twins (DTs) provide real-time synchronization between physical and virtual manufacturing systems. However, poor usability can reduce the effectiveness of any VR-based DT system (especially for users with no VR experience).

"Usability is what makes a VRDT system actually usable."

The physical twin: collaborative robots, 3D printers, conveyors, sensors, and real-time monitoring devices is designed in Unity, which enables immersive visualization, interaction, and real-time system synchronization. A ZeroMQ framework handles bi-directional data transfer, so users can remotely control manufacturing operations and watch live production (printer status, location, temperature) inside VR. Study tasks included robot control, part handling, and 3D printer operation across both the virtual and physical systems.

06

Usability & Workload Findings

✅

Good Overall Usability

Mean SUS score of 75.3 indicates good overall usability and positive user acceptance of the VRDT system

🎮

VR Familiarity Matters

Users with greater VR familiarity experienced lower workload and substantially higher usability scores

👥

No Age Effect

No significant usability differences were observed across age groups

🧩

Interface Complexity

Users found the VRDT reasonably easy to learn, though some perceived interface complexity and needed additional guidance

Task difficulty stayed moderate overall, but difficulty patterns for Tasks 3, 10, and 12 were consistent across participants: pointing to specific interface-level usability issues rather than random variation. Interaction-log analysis found little relationship between how often users interacted and how usable or workload-heavy they perceived the system to be; instead, excessive repeated interactions by some users signaled interface confusion and feedback limitations. Clustering user behavior revealed distinct groups with high, moderate, and low usability experiences, and higher perceived workload was consistently associated with lower usability. Participants also reported strong immersion and presence in the VR environment, even while reporting lower task control during interaction.
